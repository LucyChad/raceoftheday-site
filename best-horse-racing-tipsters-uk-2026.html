What to look for. What to avoid. And an honest assessment of the verified services currently operating in the UK market.

The problem with most tipster services

The UK horse racing tipster market is large, largely unregulated, and full of services that present favourable numbers. Understanding what those numbers actually mean - and which ones to look for before subscribing - is the only reliable way to evaluate any service.

Most tipster review sites make money from referral commissions. A five-star review often reflects a commercial relationship, not an independent assessment. This guide uses no paid placements and no affiliate arrangements with the services reviewed.

A tipster that only shows you winning months is not showing you its record. The record is all of it.

What to look for before subscribing

  • Full P&L published openly. Not just the winning months. The complete history, including the losing runs. Any service that selectively publishes is telling you something with that selection.
  • Third-party verification or clear methodology. A proofed record with a verifiable third party is the gold standard. A service that publishes its own results with full transparency - every selection, every result - is the next best thing. Screenshots of betting slips are not a proofed record.
  • Realistic strike rates and price ranges. A 40% strike rate is achievable only at very short prices where the P&L is negative or marginal. Services claiming 30-40% winners at odds of 3/1+ should be treated with scepticism until the full staking history is published.
  • Stated bet types and recommended stakes. Win, each-way, or both? What staking plan? Level stakes is the cleanest method. Services using variable staking or Betfair SP without stated methodology are harder to evaluate independently.
  • Accessible trial period. A service that will not let you see the record before subscribing is not a service worth trusting. The record should be available before you commit.
  • Volume of selections. High-volume services (10+ per day) are effectively portfolio builders. Low-volume services (2-7 per day) are making more targeted claims that are either right or wrong. The volume affects how quickly you can meaningfully evaluate performance.

What the numbers mean

Strike rate measures how often the tipster selects winners as a percentage of all selections. A 20% strike rate means one in five selections wins. On its own this number means very little - it depends entirely on the average odds. At evens (2.0), 20% winners would be a losing proposition. At 5/1 (6.0), 20% winners would be profitable at level stakes.

Points profit (P&L) measures the return in points per selection. One point is whatever unit stake you choose. A service showing +80 points over 12 months means that if you staked £10 per selection, you would have made £800 on top of staking costs. The number only has meaning alongside the total number of selections made - +80 points from 50 selections is a very different proposition to +80 points from 800 selections.

Return on investment (ROI) combines the two into a percentage. An ROI of 8% means for every £100 staked you returned £108. Consistently positive ROI over 500+ selections is the benchmark for a service with a demonstrable edge.

The account restriction question

Any bettor following a tipster service long-term will encounter bookmaker account restrictions. This is not unique to tipster followers - successful bettors across all disciplines face restriction. It is worth understanding before committing to any service.

Bookmakers restrict accounts that consistently bet at value prices, particularly on selections that win. Restrictions typically manifest as maximum stake limits rather than outright bans. The most common practical response is to spread activity across multiple accounts and to use exchanges (Betfair, Smarkets) for volume where SP is acceptable. Some tipster services specifically address this in their methodology guidance; others do not.
