The operation at Lodge Hill

Dan Skelton trains from Lodge Hill in Shelfield, Warwickshire - a yard he built from scratch after leaving Paul Nicholls' operation in 2013. In a little over a decade, he has transformed it into the pre-eminent British jumps stable, winning the British Champion Trainer title and becoming the principal domestic rival to the Irish yards that have come to dominate jump racing at the highest level.

The operation is a family business in the same sense as the Mullins yard. Brother Harry Skelton is the stable jockey, and the partnership has become one of the most recognisable in British racing. When Skelton sends a horse to a festival with Harry aboard, it rarely goes as a token runner.

The jockey relationship - Harry Skelton

Harry Skelton was Champion Jockey in Britain in the 2020-21 season and remains one of the most effective riders in the weighing room. His style is well-suited to the Skelton horses - particularly patient on chasers, effective at conserving horses through the early stages. The Skelton-Skelton combination is the most reliable signal from the yard that a horse is expected to perform. When Harry rides a Skelton runner at a Grade 1 meeting, the combination warrants attention regardless of price.

Dan Skelton also uses conditional riders and apprentices strategically at lower-grade meetings, which can offer value opportunities when the first string is clear but the market has not fully differentiated.

The 2026 Grand National

Panic Attack, sent off at 7/1 and considered one of the leading British hopes in the 2026 Randox Grand National, fell during the race. Rated 147 and trained specifically for Aintree, the horse was a genuine contender. The fall came at one of the earlier fences and was not a jumping error that would materially change the assessment of the horse's prospects over the National course in future.

Panic Attack fell at 7/1. The horse was right in the market for a reason. A fall at Aintree does not rewrite the ability profile - it is a fall at Aintree.

Skelton's Aintree record across 2024-2026 shows +29.85 level stake profit from 92 runners at 14% win rate - one of the strongest profitable course records in the data. His horses consistently run above expectation at Aintree relative to their starting prices.

Reading the course data

The A/E (Actual to Expected) figure tells you whether Skelton's runners outperform their market price. An A/E above 1.0 means wins are arriving at a higher rate than odds suggest - the market is consistently underestimating the horse. An A/E below 1.0 means the market has overpriced the horses relative to their actual performance.

Newton Abbot (A/E 1.37), Kelso (1.34), Newcastle (1.27), Fakenham (1.22) and Cheltenham (1.17) are all courses where Skelton runners outperform their prices. At Cheltenham in particular - the track that matters most for premium jumps racing - the yard's A/E of 1.17 from 155 runners is a meaningful signal. The market is not fully pricing in the yard's Cheltenham performance.

Warwick is the notable exception. High volume (163 runners), respectable win percentage (23%), but negative A/E (0.92) and a level-stake loss of 33.20 points. His home course. The market knows Shelfield horses well - possibly too well. Warwick is where blind following does not pay.

What Skelton's record means for betting

Three practical signals come from the data. First: Cheltenham A/E of 1.17 is persistent enough to matter. When Skelton sends runners to the Festival, they tend to outperform their prices. The market habitually underrates him at Cheltenham relative to the Irish-trained horses that dominate the market.

Second: Aintree is the most profitable course in the dataset on level stakes. The 14% win rate looks modest but has produced +29.85 points across 92 runners over two years. Panic Attack's fall in 2026 does not change this picture - it contributes to it. The underlying performance rate is strong.

Third: Harry Skelton as jockey is the clearest first-string signal from the yard. When Harry is booked, the horse is expected to run to its ability. Skelton also uses other riders, including conditionals, when filling out entries or testing younger horses. The jockey booking is the most reliable form line you will get before the race.

Cheltenham A/E 1.17 from 155 runners. The festival market consistently underprices the Shelfield yard relative to results.

The Nicholls comparison

Dan Skelton learned his trade under Paul Nicholls at Ditcheat and the influence shows. Both yards prioritise chasers over hurdlers at the highest level, both have strong records at Cheltenham and Aintree, and both operate with a clear stable jockey who is also a public identity for the brand. Nicholls has the longer record and greater depth of Grade 1 horses; Skelton's yard has grown quickly and is now considered the second British operation, ahead of Henderson in terms of direct festival threat.

For a bettor, the two yards offer different things. Nicholls horses are more thoroughly priced by the market - his runners rarely appear at genuine value. Skelton runners still attract market inefficiency, particularly at Cheltenham, which the A/E data confirms.
