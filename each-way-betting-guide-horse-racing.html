Each-way betting in horse racing: the complete guide

How each-way bets work, when they add value, when they do not, and how to calculate your return before you place the bet.

What is an each-way bet?

An each-way bet is two bets placed simultaneously for the same amount: a win bet and a place bet. If you place a £10 each-way bet, you are staking £20 in total - £10 on the horse to win, and £10 on the horse to be placed.

If the horse wins, you collect on both parts of the bet. If the horse is placed but does not win, you lose the win part and collect only on the place part. If the horse is unplaced, you lose both parts of the bet.

The place terms - how many places are paid out and at what fraction of the win odds - vary depending on the race and the bookmaker. Those terms are what determine whether an each-way bet represents value.

Place terms explained

Standard place terms for UK horse racing are as follows:

Runners Places paid Place fraction Race type
2–4 runners Win only N/A Any
5–7 runners 2 places 1/4 odds Any (excl. handicap)
8–11 runners 3 places 1/5 odds Non-handicap
8–11 runners 3 places 1/4 odds Handicap
12–15 runners 3 places 1/4 odds Handicap
16+ runners 4 places 1/4 odds Handicap
Grand National 6 places 1/4 odds Aintree handicap chase

Some bookmakers offer enhanced place terms - 5 places at 1/4 odds for big handicaps, or 1/3 odds for selected races. These enhanced terms change the value calculation significantly.

When each-way betting adds value

Each-way betting makes mathematical sense when the place fraction multiplied by the win odds produces a place odds that exceeds the fair place probability of the horse. This sounds technical but it has a practical shortcut.

The general rule: each-way value exists when the horse is 5/1 or bigger and genuine enough to place regularly, in a race with competitive place terms.

The price threshold

At 5/1 with 1/4 place odds, the place return is 5/4 (1.25/1). That means you need the horse to place at a rate exceeding approximately 44% to show a long-term place profit. In a competitive field where the horse has genuine claims, this is achievable. At 7/2 with 1/4 place odds, the place return is 7/8 - below evens. You need the horse to place more than half the time to break even on the place part alone.

Short-priced horses (below 5/1) backed each-way in standard each-way races are almost never value. The place fraction does not compensate for the reduction in odds relative to the win probability. You are better off backing the horse to win at full price.

Large handicaps and big-field races

Each-way bets gain value in large fields where place terms extend to four or more places. The Grand National (34 runners, 6 places at 1/4 odds) is the most prominent example. A horse at 20/1 in the National, backed each-way, collects 5/1 on the place part if it finishes in the first six. Given the unpredictability of the race and the number of places paid, each-way betting on genuine National contenders rated 150+ is a rational strategy, particularly on horses considered to have good jumping profiles and sound completion records.

Enhanced place terms

Many bookmakers offer enhanced place terms for selected high-profile races - five places instead of four for the Grand National, or 1/3 odds instead of 1/4 for Cheltenham Festival races. These enhanced terms change the value calculation and can make each-way betting viable at shorter prices. Checking which bookmakers offer the best place terms before placing is worth the time.

When each-way betting does not add value

Short-priced horses in small fields

A 9/4 favourite in an eight-runner handicap with three places at 1/5 odds. Place return: 9/20. You are collecting less than half the win price if the horse places but does not win, and you have doubled your stake. The maths does not work. Back the horse to win or do not back it.

Races with poor place terms

Non-handicap races with 8-11 runners at 1/5 odds rather than 1/4 provide significantly worse place value. Two places instead of three for five-to-seven runner races means a substantial reduction in place probability coverage. Always check terms before placing.

Races where you genuinely think the horse will win

If your assessment is that a horse is a clear standout selection that should win the race outright, backing each-way reduces your return on the winning scenario by forcing half the stake onto the place part at 1/4 of win odds. A horse you think will win at 6/1 is worth more backing to win than each-way. Each-way is most appropriate where you believe in the horse's class and ability but have genuine uncertainty about whether it will win specifically.

The Rule 4 deduction

If a horse is withdrawn before a race (non-runner), bookmakers apply a Rule 4 deduction to all outstanding bets on the race. The deduction percentage depends on the price of the non-runner - a short-priced non-runner causes a larger deduction. Rule 4 deductions apply to both parts of an each-way bet, win and place. This can materially affect your return if a non-runner is declared after you have placed a bet.

Practical note: Betfair and other exchanges do not operate traditional each-way markets in the same way. On exchanges, win markets and place markets are separate, allowing you to assess and price each independently. Experienced bettors often find more value in treating win and place as separate decisions on exchanges rather than as bundled each-way bets with bookmakers.
