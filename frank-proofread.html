I Am Maximus

A ten-year-old French-bred chaser rated 168 — the highest-rated horse in the race. Won with authority under Paul Townend, Mullins' retained jockey. The ride was patient through the early stages and decisive from the final few fences. Townend did not need to do anything exceptional — the horse was good enough.

Mullins ran eight horses. Won with the favourite. Had the 5th (High Class Hero, 66/1). Two unseated, two pulled up, one fell. The race was shaped by his yard.

Willie Mullins — the scale of the operation

That level of representation is without precedent in the modern era of the Grand National.

Panic Attack

Dan Skelton's Panic Attack (7/1) fell during the race. The ten-year-old was second favourite and one of the leading British hopes. Falls at Aintree are part of the race's character. Panic Attack's ability profile remains intact and he is a realistic 2027 National contender.

Iroko, Jordans and the places

Iroko (FR), trained by Oliver Greenall and Josh Guerriero and ridden by Jonjo O'Neill Jr, finished second at 18/1. The eight-year-old handled the National course well. Stablemate Jagwar (17/2) unseated early — Greenall and Guerriero will feel the result could have looked very different had both completed.

Jordans (FR) in third at 28/1, trained by Joseph O'Brien and ridden by Ben Jones, was a notable result — a seven-year-old finishing third in the Grand National is unusual. The race traditionally favours older, more experienced horses. Johnnywho (IRE) ran a solid fourth at 12/1 for the Jonjo O'Neill yard.

Best Horse Racing Tipster Services UK 2026

raceoftheday.co.uk/best-horse-racing-tipsters-uk-2026

What to look for. What to avoid. And an honest assessment of the verified services operating in the UK market in 2026. No paid placements.

The problem with most tipster services

The UK horse racing tipster market is large, largely unregulated, and full of services presenting favourable numbers. Most tipster review sites make money from referral commissions — a five-star review often reflects a commercial relationship, not an independent assessment. This guide uses no paid placements.

A tipster that only shows you winning months is not showing you its record. The record is all of it.

What to look for

  • Full P&L published openly. Not just winning months — the complete history including losing runs.
  • Third-party verification or clear methodology. A proofed record is the gold standard. Full transparent self-publication is acceptable. Screenshots are not.
  • Realistic strike rates. 15–30% depending on price range. Claims of 35%+ at average odds above 4/1 require scepticism.
  • Stated bet types and stakes. Level stakes is the cleanest method. Variable staking without stated methodology is harder to evaluate.
  • Accessible trial period. The record should be visible before you commit.
  • Volume with rationale. Services that never have a no-send day are filling space, not selecting races.

How to Choose a Verified Horse Racing Tipster

raceoftheday.co.uk/how-to-choose-a-verified-horse-racing-tipster

What verified means in practice. How to read a P&L record properly. How to identify cherry-picked results. And six questions to ask before subscribing to any service.

What "verified" actually means

The word is used loosely. In the strictest sense, a verified record is one where selections are submitted to a third party before the race — timestamped — and results recorded independently (Proform Digital, Tipster Street). This is the gold standard.

The next level: full transparent self-publication. Every selection, odds taken, result, and running P&L published openly and auditable against public race records. Not third-party verified, but verifiable.

Below that: screenshots of winning bets, highlights of profitable months, records "available on request." This is not verification. It is marketing.

Ask for the record including the losing months. If the service will not show you the losing months, you are not looking at a record. You are looking at a brochure.

Six questions to ask before subscribing

  1. Is the full selection history published openly? Every selection, every result, running P&L updated after each race. Not just winning months.
  2. What is the total number of selections over the stated period? "+120 points" means nothing without knowing how many selections produced it.
  3. What are the average odds of selections? A 25% strike rate at 3/1 is profitable. At 6/4 it is a loss. Strike rate and average odds must be read together.
  4. What bet types and staking are used? Level-stakes win bets are the cleanest method to evaluate. Variable staking without stated methodology is opaque.
  5. What odds source is used? SP is auditable. BOG produces higher returns. "Industry best odds" without a named source is unverifiable.
  6. Is there a trial period with record access before payment? A service confident in its record will let you see it before you pay.

Warning signs

Red flags

Start date that conveniently misses a losing run. Starting the record after a losing period is common.

Strike rate without average odds. A 30% strike rate at 6/4 is a losing proposition.

"100+ winners last month." High volume almost always includes winners. Volume without P&L context means nothing.

Urgency mechanics without substance. "Prices rising soon" as the primary call to action, without the record as lead content, signals where the service's confidence actually lies.

How to read a P&L record

Level stakes means the same amount on every selection. One point = whatever unit you choose (commonly £10). +80 points over 12 months means £800 profit if staking £10 per selection.

ROI combines P&L with total staking. 8% ROI means for every £100 staked you returned £108. 500+ selections is generally considered the minimum sample for meaningful conclusions.

Losing runs are normal. A system that claims otherwise is mathematically impossible over any meaningful sample. Flat periods of 8–15 consecutive selections without a winner are typical for a legitimate service. The question is whether the service communicates them honestly.

Each-Way Betting in Horse Racing — Complete Guide

raceoftheday.co.uk/each-way-betting-guide-horse-racing

How each-way bets work. Place terms by race type. When each-way adds value. When it doesn't. With worked calculations at three outcomes.

What is an each-way bet?

An each-way bet is two bets placed simultaneously for the same amount: a win bet and a place bet. A £10 each-way bet costs £20 — £10 on the horse to win, and £10 on the horse to be placed. If the horse wins, you collect on both parts. If it places but does not win, you lose the win part and collect on the place. If unplaced, you lose both.

When each-way adds value

General rule: each-way value exists when the horse is 5/1 or bigger and genuine enough to place regularly, in a race with competitive place terms.

At 5/1 with 1/4 place odds, the place return is 5/4. At 7/2 with 1/4 place odds, the place return is 7/8 — below evens. Short-priced horses backed each-way in standard each-way races are almost never value. Large handicaps with four or more places (Grand National: six places at 1/4) are where each-way bets gain most value.

When each-way does not add value

Short-priced horses in small fields. A 9/4 favourite in an eight-runner race at 1/5 place odds returns 9/20 on the place. The maths does not work — back to win or don't back at all.

When you genuinely think the horse will win. Each-way splits your stake and reduces the win return. A horse you assess as a clear standout is worth more backed to win outright.

Rule 4 deductions

If a horse is withdrawn as a non-runner after you have placed your bet, a Rule 4 deduction is applied. The deduction percentage depends on the price of the non-runner. Deductions apply to both the win and place parts of an each-way bet.

On exchanges: Betfair and other exchanges do not offer traditional each-way markets — win and place markets are separate. Experienced bettors often find more value treating win and place as independent decisions on an exchange rather than as bundled each-way bets with bookmakers.
